Spring Training has gone from players getting in shape after the off-season to an opportunity for social media content to encourage fan interaction. As teams gather in Florida and Arizona ahead of the March 27th Opening Day, these six weeks have evolved into a crucial social media moment. Players can build their brands, teams engage fans, and baseball culture comes alive on digital platforms in ways that don't seem possible during the high-stakes regular season.

Take Pete Alonso's arrival video, which generated over 4,100 likes and sparked dozens of comments analyzing his appearance. This wouldn't happen during a mid-season series against division rivals, but this unique few weeks allows for casual glimpses behind the scenes that fans crave.

Teams have recognized this opportunity, with official accounts significantly increasing their behind-the-scenes content during February and March. The Los Angeles Dodgers, for instance, regularly share pitcher bullpen sessions from Camelback Ranch, while the Yankees produce "mic'd up" content with stars like Aaron Judge during batting practice. Clearly, this type of content would be impractical during the intensity of playoff races.

Players themselves have also taken advantage of this relaxed vibe.

Mets shortstop Francisco Lindor has used Spring Training to showcase his vibrant personality through Instagram Stories, giving fans locker room tours and highlighting his pre-season routines. Meanwhile, young prospects fighting for roster spots use platforms like TikTok to document their journeys, creating personal brands before they've even secured a major league position.

Spring Training's social media impact extends far beyond the ballpark walls. While only a small percentage of fans can travel to Arizona or Florida to watch practices and games in person, social platforms have transformed the Spring Training experience.

The viral nature of this content was illustrated last month when Dodgers superstar Shohei Ohtani pulled off an elaborate car prank on manager Dave Roberts. The video, which showed Roberts being confused by a toy Porsche and a locked BMW, racked up over 6.2 million views within days of being posted to the team's official X account.

About this story

This article was developed through analysis of publicly available social media content from MLB teams and players during the 2025 Spring Training period. Primary sources included official team accounts on X (formerly Twitter), Instagram, and TikTok, with particular focus on content from the New York Mets and Los Angeles Dodgers. Engagement metrics were collected directly from these platforms, with specific attention to view counts, likes, comments, and shares. The Pete Alonso example was sourced from SNY's official X account, while the Shohei Ohtani car prank information came from reporting by Athlon Sports. Players' social media activities were observed directly from their verified accounts. No proprietary data or private information was used in the creation of this article, and all quoted material is from public posts or published reports.
